:blibb a Content Manager for humans

by ipedrazas 2013-03-25 CMS

CMS is not a web design tool, CMS is not a magic factory to produce websites. CMS systems allow you to manage your content (not your website, do yo see the difference?). Unfortunately for us, humans, there’s nothing that allow us to manage our online content as consumers.

WordPress is great, suddenly the author of this blog can swap almost any little piece of this blog with a few clicks… however, the same author is really struggling to manage his bookmarks, rss feeds (now that Google Reader is walking the green mile), pictures, music, and the issue goes on.

Haven’t you notice? it is your content, but it has turn into their business!

So, let me ask you this: when, as users, did we decide to delegate the responsibility of managing our on-line content on third parties that did whatever they liked / wanted?

Companies that when their time arrived, they left us all by the side of the road looking confused, lost and sad.

I agree, del.icio.us was great.. until they died. Nobody thought that Google would kill Reader but they did and now that uneasy feeling of me delegating my on-line content on third parties is turning into a terrible itch. My on-line resources are part of my business.

I am who I am and I do what I do because of my information.

Reader’s Assassination is the last straw. My content cannot be left in hands of companies that look at me as their way of making money. Turning Users into a comodity is a high risk game. I agree, not many people will desert all these Free Services (because they are free), but every time you use a free service remind yourself the price you will have to pay to move away. Sometimes, paying is the cheapest way to freedom.

Call me stupid but I’ve been building a CMS. A different kind of CMS, a CMS that allows me to manage my content. Perhaps it’s not as sparkly and shiny as all these other services, but look at this: it’s mine. I have total control over it. My content is nobody’s business any more.

This CMS is called :blibb and it’s a bit early to open it up to the masses but if you are interested on trying a very early version just let me know.

Secret Valentine – Results

by ipedrazas 2013-02-20 Android Android Idea stats

Let’s get into the story. Last week I published a very little app that the only thing that did was to send a (not very pretty, have to reckon) message and a picture by email.

Total numbers:

  • 30 installations in 7 countries (UK, Spain, USA, Ireland, Netherlands, Panama, Ukraine)
  • 89 Valentines sent
  • 8 Uninstalls (after 14th of February)
  • 78 visits to the app page (78.71% from Twitter, 8.17% from Facebook, 0% from G+).

I used Karmacrazy to check how many people went to the Play page. In case you don’t know, Google doesn’t let you track how many people visit your app’s page. But as I said, no analytics? no problem. In fact, you can take a look to the link stats to see the kind of information you can get from this tool.

If you want to give it a go, use this link.

More numbers. Android Versions. And this is kind of a surprise, because in the last Droidcon we talked loooong time about legacy android versions and here we have the proof of what’s the biggest problem with Android so far.

As you can see the top version is the very old 2.3.x followed by all the 4s. I fact, if we unify all the Jelly Bean devices we see that 75% of the devices are running Jelly Bean. However, who’s going to shut the door to 30% of their potential customers?

For those who are prone to slag Android think about it. Updates don’t come from Google, updates come from your carriers. The more the Android device has been customised, the harder it will be to update. Why so many devices still on 2.3.x ? because of the 24m contracts, of course.

Why Project Managers should have children

by ipedrazas 2013-02-19 startups startups anti-patterns

But there’s a particular case that gest the whole thing wrong: Parents with only one child. They think that one child takes so much time, that 3 will take at least 3 times more.

Very Wrong.

Children, like a team, don’t add up but mix. It’s all about how well organised you are and don’t get me wrong, it’s not about planning 2 weeks ahead, it’s about planning what are they going to do next or tomorrow, not in 2 weeks, or summer. Yes, registering for summer camps, like managing training of your team has to be done with plenty of time.

Managing software projects and managing children is quite similar. There are very big unknowns. Planning two weeks ahead makes not much sense. Alas, planning what are they going to do after school is never easy because you never know how tired they will be, or which mood they will come out with… Plan this big dinner to find out that they had this massive Birthday party and they’ve been fuelled by sugar and noise… So, yes, you have to plan, planning is key… as much as being able to be flexible an improvise.

How? software development left in hands of improvisation? well, don’t get me wrong, it’s not the improvisation that comes with “Don’t have a clue… let’s try this” but the improvisation that comes with experience of being now and there. Key is to plan this or that and if it fails (for any reason) do that other thing or try this other approach.

So, here we are, it’s not about reading the right books, it’s about doing the wrong thing and learning from it. Notice the LEARN bit… any parent with children know their children very well, so when they come home with a foul mood, or tired or XXXX they know how to smooth the transition from school to bed, or how I tell my wife, Bed deployment was success. All unit tests are green, house is quiet.

She text back something along the lines “nerd! … love you”

Life is never easy, but it’s about being flexible
